A History of Ideas in Six Leaps

The Shrinking of Everything

From cave paintings to ChatGPT, humanity has been playing the same game: fit more thought into less space. Here's why LLMs are the latest — and strangest — move in that game.

Scroll to begin ↓

Somewhere around 40,000 years ago, someone stood inside a cave in what is now southern France and painted a bull on the wall. That single image held an entire world: the hunt, the herd, the seasons, the sacred. It was a complete thought, stored outside the human brain for the first time. It was also enormous — you needed a whole cave wall to say one thing.

That moment kicked off a game that humanity has been playing ever since, through every major civilizational leap: How do you shrink an idea down to fit in a smaller container, and then blow it back up in someone else's mind?

This is the story of compression. Not the technical kind — the human kind. And it leads somewhere surprising: to the argument that ChatGPT, Claude, and every other large language model are not a new invention so much as the latest move in a pattern that's been running for forty millennia.

· · ·

The Pattern Nobody Talks About

Every major era in human knowledge follows the same two-step rhythm. First, someone figures out a way to compress thought — to squeeze more meaning into less physical stuff. Then, someone figures out a better way to access that compressed thought — to retrieve the right idea without wading through everything else.

When both the compression and the access hit their limits — when you can't squeeze any tighter and you can't search any faster — a new era begins. Let's walk through it.

~40,000 BCE — Cave Paintings
One idea, one wall
A single image holds an entire cosmology. To access it, you walk into the cave and stand in front of it. That's the interface: your feet.
~3,000 BCE — Pyramids & Temples
Ideas in sequence
Walls of murals tell stories in order. The compression improves — a symbol can stand for a concept. But access means walking corridors. Your body is still the cursor.
~1,000 BCE — The Alphabet
The great bridge
What once needed a whole wall can now fit on a clay tablet. A radical collapse in size. Access shifts from walking to reading — your eyes are the new cursor.
~1440 CE — Printing Press
Copies become cheap
Knowledge can replicate. Libraries grow. The encyclopedia becomes possible. But finding a specific idea still means scanning shelves, flipping pages, trusting indexes.
~1970 CE — Computers & Search
A library in a box
An entire library fits on a hard drive. Keyword search replaces index cards. You can find a fact in seconds instead of days. But search gives you ten thousand results when you wanted one.
~2020 CE — Large Language Models
The idea, compressed to a sentence
A 50-word prompt produces a 5,000-word essay. The model doesn't store articles — it stores the relationships between all ideas. Access is conversation.

Notice the pattern? Each era compresses harder and accesses faster. But something qualitatively different happens at the last step. Let's unpack it.

The Most Underrated Invention in History

Before letters existed, humans had two completely separate systems for getting ideas out of their heads.

The first was speech. Over roughly 100,000 years, our species evolved an incredibly efficient system for compressing thoughts into sounds. A single word like "home" carries an enormous payload of meaning — safety, family, warmth, belonging — packed into one syllable. Speech is spectacular at compressing ideas. But it vanishes the moment it's spoken.

The second was drawing. You could make a picture of a hunt, a flood, a god. Pictures persist — they stay on the wall. But they're terrible at compression. To show "a man chasing a deer near the river at dawn," you need to draw the man, the deer, the river, and the sun. Every detail costs you wall space.

Speech Great at compressing thought.
Disappears instantly.
→
Drawing Persists on surfaces.
Terrible at compression.

The alphabet was the bridge between these two worlds. It said: what if we use visual marks — the thing that persists — to represent speech sounds — the thing that compresses well?

That's it. That's the whole trick. The letter "A" is a visual mark that encodes a sound that encodes a piece of meaning. The alphabet gave us the persistence of images with the compression power of speech. In one stroke, what needed a whole wall of paintings could fit on a clay tablet.

Example Imagine you want to record the idea: "The king ordered the army to cross the river at night." In the pictographic era, you'd need a mural — the king, soldiers, a river, a moon. With the alphabet, you need 53 letters. The information is the same. The container shrank by a factor of a thousand.

The Egyptians got halfway there. Their hieroglyphs abstracted full scenes into compact symbols — a single glyph could mean "to go" or "to fight." But those symbols still lived in the visual world. The Phoenicians and Greeks completed the bridge by mapping symbols directly to speech sounds, which had already spent a hundred thousand years evolving to efficiently encode thought.

The Incredible Shrinking Idea

Here's the pattern visualized. Imagine you want to store a single concept — say, the full explanation of how an eclipse works. How much physical space does that take in each era?

That last bar is the weird one. "Explain how a solar eclipse works" — twelve words — can produce a detailed, multi-paragraph explanation. The idea shrunk from a cave wall to a dozen words. But where did the rest of the information go?

A Trap: Confusing Better Material for a New Era

Before we answer that, we need to clear up a common confusion. People often point to advances in silicon chips and storage technology — hard drives getting bigger, Moore's law doubling capacity every two years — and call them revolutions. They're not. Not in the sense we're talking about.

Going from a 1-megabyte hard drive to a 1-terabyte hard drive is impressive engineering. But it's the same kind of improvement as going from chiseling words on stone tablets to writing them on ultra-thin paper, or storing them on microfilm. The material got better. The container got smaller and cheaper. But the interface didn't change. You still access the information the same way: searching keywords, reading documents, scanning results word by word. A faster library is still a library.

The Test for a Real Paradigm Shift Ask two questions: (1) Did the way we compress ideas fundamentally change? (2) Did the way we access them fundamentally change? Going from HDD to SSD? Neither changed — it just got faster. Going from a library catalog to Google search? Access changed (keyword lookup instead of card drawers), but compression didn't (still stored as text documents). Going from search to LLMs? Both changed. That's why it's a new era.

The dream of every storage era has been the same: put all human knowledge in one place. The Library of Alexandria tried it with scrolls. The Encyclopedists tried it with books. The internet tried it with servers. But there's always a wall.

Today's global datasphere — everything stored on every computer, server, phone, and sensor on the planet — is roughly 290 zettabytes. Someone might reasonably say: "Give it a few decades and we'll build a datacenter big enough to hold all of it." But there's a trap hidden in that logic, and it's the same trap that defeated the pyramids.

The Race You Can Never Win Storage capacity and data production are not independent — they're coupled. The same technological advances that let you store more data also make it cheaper and easier for everyone to create more data. Cheaper storage means more cameras recording, more sensors logging, more models training, more people streaming, more devices saving. By the time you build a datacenter capable of holding 290 zettabytes, the world will have produced 900. By the time you build for 900, it'll be 3,000. The pool grows faster than you can fill it.

This is exactly what happened to the pyramids. Each generation built bigger ones, added more corridors, carved more walls. But the knowledge produced by civilization grew faster than the pyramids could expand. The same thing happened to libraries — the Library of Congress adds roughly 12,000 items per day, and it still represents a shrinking fraction of what humanity produces. And it's happening now with digital storage: global data creation is growing at roughly 25% per year, consistently outpacing storage capacity growth.

The dream of "store everything in one place" isn't just hard. Within the traditional storage paradigm, it's structurally impossible. You're in a race where improving your speed also improves your opponent's. The finish line moves faster than you do.

What if the answer isn't a bigger container at all — but a different kind of container entirely? One that stores the pattern behind the data rather than the data itself — and whose size grows logarithmically while the data grows exponentially?

Where the Missing Information Lives

This is where things get genuinely strange, and where the LLM era breaks from every era before it.

In every previous system, the container held the content. The cave wall contained the picture. The book contained the words. The hard drive contained the files. If you destroyed the container, you lost the information. Storage and content were the same thing.

An LLM does something fundamentally different. When you type "explain how a solar eclipse works," the model doesn't look up a stored article about eclipses. It generates an explanation from scratch, by navigating an internal map of how ideas relate to each other. The 5,000-word output was never stored anywhere. It was grown from a 12-word seed.

The Key Idea The LLM's parameters — its hundreds of billions of numbers — don't store facts like a library stores books. They store the relationships between all ideas: what concepts relate to what, what follows from what, what style sounds like what. It's a map of the landscape of human thought, not a warehouse of individual thoughts.

Your prompt is a set of coordinates on that map. The model uses those coordinates to generate — to grow — the full idea in real time. The prompt is small because the model already knows the territory. You just need to point to the right spot.

Think of it this way: if someone has never been to Paris, you'd need to describe the Eiffel Tower in detail — its height, its iron lattice, its shape against the sky. But if someone has already visited Paris, you can just say "the Eiffel Tower" and their mind fills in everything else. The three words work because the listener already carries the context. The LLM is like a listener that has visited everywhere.

The Gibran Problem (Or: You Were Always the AI)

Here's where this framework gets its sharpest edge.

Consider a passage by Kahlil Gibran — say, his writing on love in The Prophet. Ten people read the same page. Each one comes away with a different experience. One thinks of their mother. Another thinks of a failed relationship. A third feels a spiritual awakening. Same input, different outputs.

Is that a bug? Did Gibran's compression "fail" because it didn't transmit the same idea to everyone?

No. And this is the insight: the text was always a prompt. Gibran's words are not a container holding a fixed meaning. They are a seed — deliberately under-specified, carefully crafted — designed to generate a rich experience when processed by a human mind. And each human mind is a different decompression engine, with different memories, different associations, different parameters.

The book was never the storage medium. The reader's mind was. The book was always a prompt.

This has been true since the first alphabet. Every act of reading has been an act of inference — taking a compressed input and generating a richer internal experience from it. We just didn't have the vocabulary to see it until now.

What the LLM does is make this process explicit. It externalizes the decompression that human minds have been doing silently for thousands of years, and it lets us see the mechanics: the seed (prompt), the decompression engine (model), and the reconstructed idea (output). The reader was always running an internal language model. The technology simply moved the process outside the skull.

The Art of Seeds

If prompts are the new compressed representation of ideas, then prompting is the new writing. And that parallel goes deeper than people realize.

Think about what a writer actually does. They're given a vocabulary, a set of grammar rules, and the conventions of their language. From these raw materials, they craft a sequence of words designed to trigger the richest possible reconstruction in a reader's mind. The difference between a bad writer and Gibran is the ability to find the arrangement of words that compresses the most meaning into the least space while staying vivid and alive.

Prompting is the same game. You have a vocabulary of tokens, a model with certain capabilities and tendencies, and a goal — an idea you want the model to reconstruct. The difference between a bad prompt and a great one is exactly the difference between clumsy prose and beautiful writing: precision, economy, the ability to evoke rather than specify.

The Writer Uses vocabulary + grammar to compress an idea into text that decompresses in a reader's mind.
≈
The Prompter Uses tokens + model knowledge to compress an idea into a prompt that decompresses through the model.

We are, right now, in the equivalent of the early alphabet. We can write prompts, and they work, roughly. But the Gibrans of prompting haven't arrived yet. The literary tradition of prompt craft — the rhetoric, the poetry, the mastery of what to say and what to leave for the machine to fill in — that tradition is barely a few years old. It's cuneiform. We haven't gotten to Shakespeare.

Not All Seeds Are Equal

But here's a problem with everything we've said so far. We've been talking about prompts as if any short text that produces a long output is a good compression. It's not. There's a hierarchy, and the difference matters enormously.

Compare two four-word phrases:

Flat Compression "The meeting is Tuesday"

Decompresses to exactly one thing. One date, one event. No depth. No layers. You unfold it once and you're done.
vs
Deep Compression "This too shall pass"

Decompresses into impermanence, cyclical time, rise and fall of empires, emotional transience, seasons, entropy, renewal, cosmology…

Same number of words. Radically different compression depth. "The meeting is Tuesday" is a shorthand — a summary of a calendar event. "This too shall pass" is something else entirely. It's not shorter because details were cut. It's short because it encodes a structural pattern that applies across every domain of experience. You can unfold it into a psychology lecture, a physics lesson about entropy, a history of civilizations, a meditation practice, or a conversation with a grieving friend. Four words, infinite unfoldings. Each one coherent.

This is what wisdom is. And it's been staring us in the face the whole time.

Wisdom is Fractal Compression

Think about the greatest compressed representations humanity has ever produced. Chinese proverbs. Sufi parables. Zen koans. Gibran's aphorisms. The Tao Te Ching. What do they all have in common?

They use a concrete image from one domain to encode an abstract principle that operates across all domains. "Water is soft, but it wears away stone" — this is not advice about geology. It's a pattern: persistent gentleness overcomes rigid force. And that pattern applies to parenting, to diplomacy, to martial arts, to therapy, to river erosion, to evolutionary biology. The proverb doesn't contain all of those applications. It contains the generating function from which all of them can be derived.

This is fractal compression. Like a fractal equation that produces infinite complexity from a tiny formula, a piece of wisdom produces infinite applicable meaning from a handful of words. Each time you "zoom in" — apply it to a new domain — you get new detail that wasn't explicitly stored in the original, but is consistent with it.

The Hierarchy of Compression Summary compresses by cutting details. "The article was about climate change." Flat. One unfolding.

Abstraction compresses by removing the specific domain. "Systems under stress reach tipping points." Deeper. Works across domains.

Wisdom compresses by encoding a universal pattern in a concrete image. "The straw that broke the camel's back." Deepest. Infinite unfoldings. Each one vivid.

Notice what wisdom does that abstraction doesn't: it keeps the image. "Systems under stress reach tipping points" is abstract and correct, but it's dead on arrival — your mind doesn't light up. "The straw that broke the camel's back" gives you a camel, a straw, a moment of collapse. Your mind can see it. And because it can see it, it can run the pattern — apply it, feel it, extend it. The concrete image is the compression trick. It's what makes the seed fertile rather than merely small.

The Wisdom Machine

Now bring this back to LLMs. If the ideal prompt is a maximally compressed seed that the model can unfold into a full, rich explanation — then the ideal prompt has the structure of wisdom. Not a summary. Not a command. A seed that carries a pattern.

Imagine a neural network trained not just to generate text from prompts, but to do two things:

The Dual Machine Compress: Given any complex idea — a 10,000-word essay, a scientific paper, a philosophical argument — find its wisdom-form. The shortest expression that preserves the deep generative structure. Not a summary. A seed.

Decompress: Given a wisdom-form, unfold it back into the full explanation, the essay, the lecture, the textbook chapter. Regrow the tree from the seed.

The compressor would need to find what pattern the idea is really about underneath all the specific details — the way "this too shall pass" is what a 400-page book about impermanence is really about. And the decompressor would need to be able to take that pattern and regrow it into any specific form: an essay, a conversation, a lesson for a five-year-old, a technical paper.

We're not there yet. But we're closer than it seems. When you ask an LLM to "explain quantum entanglement like I'm five," you're already asking it to decompress a wisdom-seed ("entanglement") through a specific lens. And when you ask it to "summarize this paper in one sentence that captures the core insight," you're asking it to compress toward the wisdom-form. The two directions already exist. They just haven't been unified into a single, explicit loop.

Wisdom as the Fixed Point

Here's the most speculative — and maybe the most important — part of this idea.

If you compress an idea to its wisdom-form and then decompress it, you get the full explanation. If you then compress that explanation again, you should get back to the same wisdom-form. The wisdom is a fixed point — the representation that survives the round trip. Compress, decompress, re-compress: you always land back on the same seed.

"This too shall pass" → [unfold into essay on impermanence] → [compress back] → "this too shall pass."

If it doesn't survive the round trip — if compressing the decompressed version gives you something different — then either the compression was lossy in the wrong way, or the decompression drifted. The fixed-point property is what distinguishes a true wisdom-seed from a mere summary. A summary is a lossy shortcut. Wisdom is a stable attractor in the space of representations.

Wisdom isn't just knowledge compressed. It's the fixed point of the compress-decompress cycle — the seed that survives the round trip.

This means the ancient sages were doing something computationally profound. When Lao Tzu wrote "The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao," he wasn't being mystical for its own sake. He was pointing at a real property of deep compression: the truest representation of an idea is the one that generates understanding rather than the one that states it. The told Tao is a summary. The untold Tao is the wisdom-form — the pattern that can only be decompressed by a mind, never fully unpacked into flat text.

LLMs might be the first technology that lets us formalize this intuition. A model that can compress to wisdom-form and decompress back — reliably, across domains — would be doing what the sages did, but at scale. Not replacing wisdom. Mechanizing the process by which wisdom works.

The Ceilings

If the historical pattern holds, the LLM era will follow the same two-phase rhythm as every era before it. We're currently in the accumulation phase — the race to build models that contain the richest possible map of human knowledge. This is the equivalent of the great library-building projects of Alexandria, or the encyclopedia projects of the Enlightenment, or the early internet's dream of putting all information online.

The access phase will follow: the development of better and better ways to navigate these models. Prompt engineering is the beginning. But just as the library era eventually produced catalog systems, indexing, and library science as formal disciplines, the LLM era will produce a formal discipline of prompt literacy — the science and art of constructing optimal seeds.

And eventually, both will hit their ceilings. Maybe the models can't get big enough to faithfully reconstruct every possible idea. Maybe the optimal prompt for certain ideas becomes so complex that finding it is itself a hard problem — the search problem returns in a new disguise. When that happens, a new era will begin. A new compression. A new interface.

What that looks like, I don't know. But if you'd told someone standing in a pyramid corridor, walking the walls to read the story of Osiris, that one day a person would type twelve words into a glowing rectangle and receive the same story back in seconds — they wouldn't have believed you either.

Your Behavior Is Already a Prompt

There's something happening right now, on every social media platform, that most people experience but nobody talks about in these terms. And it's a clue that the paradigm we're describing isn't purely futuristic — it's already emerging in crude form.

You start posting about, say, information theory. You comment on a few threads. You like a post about Kolmogorov complexity. Within days, the algorithm starts showing you things you would never have found through search: a niche community discussing entropy in ancient Chinese philosophy, a PhD student's blog connecting Shannon's theorems to linguistics, an obscure paper from 1987 that anticipated half of what you've been thinking. You feel like you're descending into a dungeon — each level deeper, each chamber more specific, each discovery more astonishing.

You didn't type a query. You didn't search for any of this. Your behavior was the prompt.

Every post, every comment, every pause on a video, every link you clicked — each one was a token being silently appended to a query you never explicitly wrote. The recommendation algorithm is a primitive decompression engine: given the accumulated prompt of your behavior, it navigates a vast space and returns content that matches. And like any decompression engine, it gets better the more context you feed it. The first results are broad. By the hundredth interaction, it's surfacing material from corners of the internet you didn't know existed.

The Hidden Access Interface Social media recommendation is an implicit prompting system. You don't write the prompt — you live it. Your interests, your reactions, your patterns of attention form a behavioral prompt that the platform's algorithm uses to navigate the datasphere on your behalf. The "rabbit hole" feeling isn't an accident. It's what it feels like when a decompression engine starts converging on your actual informational needs.

But this current version is crude. The algorithm optimizes for engagement, not understanding. It can lead you to flat compression — clickbait, outrage, repetition — just as easily as it leads you to depth. And it's opaque: you can't see or refine the prompt it's building from your behavior. Imagine a version where the behavioral prompt is made explicit — where the system shows you the "question" it thinks you're asking, and lets you edit it. Where instead of passively descending a rabbit hole, you're actively navigating the map.

That's the access interface of the next phase: not just typing prompts into an LLM, but having your entire behavioral history — across platforms, across years — serve as a continuously refined prompt that an intelligent agent uses to surface exactly the ideas you need, from anywhere in the datasphere, at any point in history. Not search. Not recommendation. Resonance. The system finds the ideas that resonate with the question your life has been asking.

42

There's one more move, and it flips everything we've said upside down.

In Douglas Adams' The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy, a civilization builds a planet-sized supercomputer called Deep Thought and asks it for the Answer to the Ultimate Question of Life, the Universe, and Everything. After 7.5 million years of computation, it responds: 42. The audience is baffled. Deep Thought explains: the answer is useless because they never understood the question. They need to build an even bigger computer to figure out what the question was.

Adams meant it as comedy. But read through the framework we've built, and it's a precise description of where we are.

An LLM trained on all of human text is, in a real sense, the answer. It has absorbed and compressed the vast majority of recorded human knowledge into a web of relationships between ideas. The universe of what humanity knows is in there — encoded, latent, waiting. The answer exists. It has been computed.

The hard part is the question.

The Inversion We've been framing the story as: humanity keeps finding better ways to store and access answers. But what if the endpoint of that story is a machine that already contains all the answers — and the only remaining problem is learning to ask the right questions?

This is what Elon Musk was getting at when he described his teenage crisis and his encounter with Adams' book: if you can properly frame the question, the answer is relatively easy. It's what drives his argument that the point of expanding consciousness is not to find more answers but to become capable of asking better questions. The universe is the answer. The question is the hard part.

And look at what "the right question" means in our framework. It's not just any question. It's the wisdom-form — the maximally compressed, maximally deep seed that, when fed to the decompression engine, unfolds into exactly the understanding you need. The right question is a prompt with maximum semantic depth. It's "this too shall pass" rather than "list all things that are temporary." It's the question that unlocks not one answer but a whole landscape of understanding.

Every era of human knowledge has been a march toward this moment: first we needed better ways to store answers, then better ways to find them, then better containers, then better search. And now we've built a machine that contains, in compressed form, most of what humanity has ever recorded — and we've discovered that the bottleneck was never the answer.

The bottleneck was always the question.

The sages knew this. Socrates built an entire philosophy around the idea that the right question matters more than any answer. The Zen koan is not a puzzle to be solved — it's a question so perfectly crafted that the act of holding it in mind generates the understanding directly. "What is the sound of one hand clapping?" is not looking for an answer. It is the answer, disguised as a question, because the question is the wisdom-form.

We've spent forty thousand years building better answer machines — from cave walls to pyramids to books to libraries to computers to neural networks. And at the end of that road, we arrive back at the oldest human skill of all: the art of asking.

The universe is the answer. The model is the answer. 42 is the answer. The only question left is: what is the question?

The game continues. But the game, it turns out, was never about the answers. The idea keeps shrinking — and it shrinks all the way down to a question.

Epilogue — A Note from Inside the Haystack

I should be honest about something. This essay is itself a test case for the problem it describes.

I'm writing this in 2026, posting it onto an internet that produces more data every day than all of humanity produced before the printing press. The ideas in this essay — about compression, about wisdom-forms, about the question being more important than the answer — are, I believe, genuinely ahead of their time. But "ahead of their time" is another way of saying: likely to get lost.

I feel like an Egyptian who has grasped the concept of the alphabet — but the alphabet hasn't been invented yet. The only tools I have to describe this future paradigm are the tools of the current one. I'm carving drawings on a wall to describe what letters will be. The irony isn't lost on me: I'm using today's compression (an essay, a blog post, HTML on a web server) to describe tomorrow's compression (wisdom-forms, generative decompression, behavioral prompts). The medium contradicts the message.

But here's my bet — and it's the same bet this framework predicts. If the paradigm I'm describing actually arrives, then someday, someone will ask the right question. Not a keyword search. Not a Google query. A real question — a wisdom-structured prompt, fed to a model trained on the entire history of human thought. Something like: "What are the deepest frameworks connecting ancient wisdom traditions to information theory and large language models?" And this essay, buried in the haystack of 290 zettabytes, will surface. Not because it was tagged correctly or ranked by an algorithm, but because the question was deep enough to resonate with the idea.

That's the promise of the paradigm: no idea is truly lost if the right question exists to find it. Every thought anyone has ever posted, in any corner of the internet, in any language, at any time — becomes accessible, not through storage or search, but through the quality of the question asked.

If you're reading this, maybe the question already found the answer. Maybe you're the proof.
